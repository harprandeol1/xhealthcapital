EB-5 for Indian Investors and NRIs

India sends more EB-5 investors every year, largely because Indian-born applicants face the longest employment-based green card backlogs of any nationality. Here's how Indian families structure a healthcare EB-5 investment, remit funds under RBI rules, and document source of funds to USCIS's standard.

🇮🇳 RBI/LRS remittance guidance 📄 ITR & gift-deed documentation support 🕐 IST-friendly consultation scheduling

Why so many Indian families look at EB-5

India-born applicants in the EB-2 and EB-3 employment categories face per-country visa caps that create backlogs often exceeding a decade — in some age brackets and categories, decades. Families with children approaching 21 face "aging out" risk, where a child loses derivative status before the family's priority date is current. EB-5, particularly through the rural TEA reserved visa pool, has generally processed with less country-specific congestion, making it an increasingly common option for Indian families who want a defined and independent path to a Green Card for the whole family — spouse and unmarried children under 21 included.

Moving capital out of India: RBI and LRS basics

The Reserve Bank of India's Liberalised Remittance Scheme (LRS) permits resident individuals to remit up to $250,000 per financial year for permitted current and capital account purposes, which includes overseas investment. Because the EB-5 minimum is $800,000, most Indian families use one or more of the following structures:

  • Multi-year remittance: transferring the LRS limit across two or more financial years ahead of the planned investment date.
  • Multiple family members: each Indian resident individual has their own $250,000 LRS allowance, so a principal investor, spouse, and adult children can jointly contribute toward a single investment (with appropriate gift or loan documentation between family members).
  • Funds already held offshore: NRIs and OCIs with existing foreign bank accounts, foreign business income, or foreign investment proceeds may not be subject to LRS limits for those specific funds.
  • Sale of Indian assets: property or business sale proceeds remitted under RBI's separate rules for repatriation of sale proceeds (subject to its own documentation and, often, a $1 million per financial year cap under FEMA for NRIs repatriating balances in NRO accounts).

Work with an authorized dealer bank early

Every remittance path above needs proper Form 15CA/15CB tax certification and an RBI-authorized dealer bank to process the transfer and issue a Foreign Outward Remittance Certificate (FOIRC/FIRC). Start this process months before your planned investment date — banks can take weeks to process large outward remittances with full compliance documentation.

Source-of-funds documentation Indian investors typically need

  • Income tax returns (ITRs) for the past 3–5 years for the investor and, if applicable, the gifting relative
  • Bank statements showing fund accumulation over time
  • Sale deed and capital gains documentation if funds come from a property sale
  • Audited financial statements if funds originate from a business
  • Notarized gift deed, if funds are gifted from a parent or relative, along with the donor's own source-of-funds trail
  • RBI/bank remittance certificates (Form A2, FIRC) evidencing the LRS transfer
