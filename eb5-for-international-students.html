EB-5 for International Students: Turn F1/OPT Status Into a Green Card

If you're studying in the U.S. on an F1 visa or working on OPT, EB-5 gives you a path to permanent residency that doesn't depend on an employer, a lottery, or a specific job offer — only on a qualifying investment in a healthcare project.

🎓 No employer sponsorship needed 👨‍👩‍👧 Parents/relatives can gift funds 🕒 File before OPT expires

Why students consider EB-5

F1 students face a hard deadline: OPT (and STEM OPT extensions) eventually run out, and H1B sponsorship depends on an employer entering — and winning — the annual lottery. For students from high-demand countries, even a winning H1B lottery number leads into a multi-year (sometimes multi-decade) green card backlog. EB-5 sidesteps all of that. It is not tied to a job offer, an employer's willingness to sponsor, or the H1B cap, which is why it's increasingly used by students who want certainty rather than betting their future on lottery odds.

How the timeline typically works

  1. While still in school or on OPT: confirm source-of-funds documentation (savings, parental gifts, or sale of assets) and select a healthcare project.
  2. File Form I-526E: your immigration attorney submits the immigrant petition along with the project's business plan and job-creation methodology.
  3. Maintain status: continue on F1/OPT (or transition to another valid status) while I-526E is pending — this is why filing early, well before OPT expires, matters.
  4. Adjustment of status or consular processing: once a visa number is available for your category, you and your attorney pursue adjustment of status (if inside the U.S.) or consular processing (if abroad).
  5. Conditional Green Card, then I-829: after approval, conditions are removed roughly two years later once job creation is documented.

Why timing matters for students

EB-5 processing takes time. Students closest to OPT expiration should start the source-of-funds and project-selection process as early as possible — waiting until the final months of OPT significantly narrows your options.

Funding your investment as a student

Very few students have $800,000 in personal savings, and that's expected. USCIS accepts several lawful sources of investment capital for student petitioners:

  • Gifts from parents or relatives — the most common source for student investors, provided the gift is documented with a signed gift letter and the donor can show their own lawful source of funds.
  • Sale of property or business assets owned by the student or their family.
  • Inheritance, with appropriate estate documentation.
  • Loans secured by the student's or a family member's assets (not unsecured personal loans, which USCIS scrutinizes closely).

Whatever the source, USCIS requires a documented paper trail showing the funds were obtained lawfully. See our full source-of-funds guide for documentation checklists.

Why healthcare projects fit student investors well

Because healthcare facilities carry non-discretionary local demand and staffing-heavy job creation, they tend to produce a comfortable buffer above the 10-job minimum per investor — reducing the risk that a slow lease-up or reduced patient volume threatens the job count your I-829 depends on. That's a meaningful consideration for students who may still be building a career in the U.S. and want an investment that doesn't require active management.
