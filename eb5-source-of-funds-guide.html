EB-5 Source of Funds: What USCIS Wants to See

Source of funds is the most scrutinized part of an EB-5 petition. USCIS needs a complete, traceable paper trail proving your investment capital was obtained lawfully — here's what that looks like for the most common funding sources.

The core principle

It's not enough to show you have $800,000 — you must document exactly how you earned or acquired every dollar, tracing it back to a lawful source. USCIS adjudicators will follow the money through bank statements, tax filings, and legal documents, so gaps or unexplained deposits are the most common cause of Requests for Evidence (RFEs) and denials.

Documentation by funding source

Salary / employment income

Employment verification letters, pay stubs, tax returns for multiple years, and bank statements showing the accumulation of savings over time.

Business ownership / profits

Business registration documents, audited financial statements, tax filings for the business and the individual, and bank records showing profit distributions to the investor.

Sale of property

Sale deed/agreement, proof of prior ownership, valuation or appraisal records, and bank records showing receipt of sale proceeds.

Gifted funds

A notarized gift letter/deed confirming the funds are a genuine gift (not a loan to be repaid), plus the donor's own source-of-funds documentation proving they lawfully obtained the gifted amount.

Inheritance

Death certificate, will or succession documents, probate records, and bank statements showing the transfer of inherited funds to the investor.

Loans secured by assets

Loan agreement, documentation of the collateral securing the loan (e.g., real estate), and evidence the lender is a legitimate financial institution. Unsecured personal loans are scrutinized far more heavily and are generally discouraged.

Sale of securities / investments

Brokerage statements showing purchase and sale history, and bank records showing the proceeds landing in the investor's account.

Country-specific considerations

Cross-border investors face additional documentation layers tied to their home country's currency controls and reporting requirements:

  • India: RBI/LRS remittance certificates (Form A2, FIRC), income tax returns, and gift deeds. See our Indian investor guide for details.
  • UAE/GCC: UAE bank statements and business ownership records; enhanced due diligence documentation is often requested by U.S. banks receiving large Gulf-region wires. See our Dubai & UAE guide.
  • Other countries: equivalent tax, banking, and property records translated into English with certified translations where required.

Start early

Assembling a complete, USCIS-ready source-of-funds file often takes longer than any other part of the process — especially for business income, property sales, or funds accumulated over many years. Begin gathering documentation as soon as you're seriously considering EB-5, even before selecting a specific project.

Work with an immigration attorney and, for cross-border investors, a tax advisor familiar with both your home country's regulations and USCIS documentation standards. See our program requirements guide for the broader eligibility picture.
