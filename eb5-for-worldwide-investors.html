EB-5 for Investors From Any Country

EB-5 is open to nationals of every country. While we've published detailed guides for our largest investor groups, the core process — investment, job creation, I-526E, conditional residency, I-829 — is the same regardless of where you're investing from.

Country-of-birth backlogs: what to check first

EB-5 visa availability is allocated by country of birth (not citizenship or residence), with per-country caps designed to prevent any single country from consuming the entire annual allocation. In practice, this means applicants born in mainland China face the longest-standing EB-5 backlog, and applicants born in India and, at times, Vietnam, have also experienced longer waits than the rest of the world. Investors born in most other countries — including Brazil, Nigeria, the UK, South Africa, the Philippines, and elsewhere — have generally had visas current or close to current, meaning minimal additional wait beyond standard processing.

Reserved categories, including the rural TEA set-aside, draw from a separate pool of visas specifically carved out by Congress, which has generally kept those visas available faster even for backlogged countries. Always confirm current visa bulletin data with your immigration attorney before finalizing a timeline.

What every investor needs regardless of nationality

  1. Lawful source of funds for the full investment amount, documented with a clear paper trail (see our source-of-funds guide).
  2. A qualifying healthcare project in a Targeted Employment Area for the reduced $800,000 threshold, or $1,050,000 for non-TEA projects.
  3. An independent U.S. immigration attorney to prepare and file Form I-526E.
  4. Patience through the visa bulletin process, which varies by country of birth as outlined above.

Why healthcare, no matter where you're investing from

Regardless of nationality, every EB-5 investor is underwriting the same risk: will this project create and sustain the required jobs, and will the underlying business remain viable? Healthcare facilities — serving non-discretionary community needs with staffing-heavy job creation — offer a defensible answer to both questions across economic cycles, which is why we've built our entire regional center around this single sector.
